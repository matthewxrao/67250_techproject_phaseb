Design Rationale

This document explains how my website redesign directly addresses the usability problems identified during Phase A analysis. Every major design decision is grounded in session data, funnel metrics, and observed user behavior patterns.

1. Shortening the Funnel

The Problem

At approximately 10 steps, the museum's purchase funnel was nearly double the industry benchmark of 5.1 steps. This excess length was driven primarily by extensive pre-purchase browsing rather than a lengthy checkout process itself. Users visited many informational pages before reaching, or failing to reach, the ticket purchase flow.

What I Changed

I restructured the site architecture to reduce the number of pages a user must navigate before reaching checkout. The home page now features a prominent "Buy Tickets" call-to-action directly within the hero section, allowing users to reach the purchase flow in a single click from landing. The exhibitions page also includes direct pathways to ticket purchase, eliminating the need to navigate back to a separate tickets overview page first. By consolidating informational content and surfacing the purchase entry point earlier, I reduced the realistic path-to-purchase to approximately 3-4 steps: land, browse one content page, click buy, and complete checkout.

How This Connects to the Data

With only 119 out of 1,259 sessions (9.45%) resulting in a purchase, and most drop-off occurring before users ever reached checkout, the data clearly indicated that users were getting lost in informational browsing loops. Shortening the funnel attacks the highest-volume drop-off zone directly.

2. Making "Buy Tickets" Discoverable

The Problem

The "Buy Tickets" option was hard to find. The ticket purchase call-to-action needed to be more prominent and accessible from all pages, not just the tickets overview page. Users who wanted to purchase had to actively seek out the correct page.

What I Changed

"Buy Tickets" now appears as a persistent link in the navigation bar, visible on every single page of the site. On the home page, it is additionally presented as a large, visually distinct button in the hero section. On the exhibitions page, each exhibition description is positioned near clear purchase pathways. The navigation redesign uses uppercase, well-spaced typography that gives equal visual weight to the ticket link alongside other navigation items, ensuring it is never overlooked.

How This Connects to the Data

The 9.45% conversion rate with most drop-off before checkout pointed to a discoverability problem, not a checkout problem. By placing the purchase entry point in the persistent navigation and on high-traffic pages like the home page and exhibitions, I address the exact location where the data showed users were falling out of the funnel.

3. Checkout Errors Are Not the Barrier

The Finding

Users who encountered form errors during checkout still completed their purchases at a 100% rate. While error reduction is always beneficial, the data showed this was not the most impactful area for improvement.

What I Changed

Rather than investing heavily in checkout error prevention, I focused my redesign effort on the upstream problem: getting users to checkout in the first place. The purchase form was given a clean, modern treatment with clear labels, generous spacing, and a focused single-column layout housed within a distinct card container. These improvements maintain a professional checkout experience without over-engineering a flow that the data showed was already effective at converting users who reached it.

How This Connects to the Data

The 100% error recovery rate confirmed that checkout friction was minimal. My redesign correctly prioritized upstream discoverability and funnel length over checkout micro-optimizations, aligning engineering effort with where the data showed the greatest opportunity for conversion improvement.

4. Member Conversion

The Problem

Members converted at a lower rate (7.64%) than guests (9.79%) and students (9.03%). This suggested that member-specific flows may have introduced unnecessary complexity, or that the purchase experience was not tailored to users who may already have admission included in their membership.

What I Changed

The ticket pricing table now clearly communicates that member admission is free, with a distinct row that differentiates the member benefit from paid ticket types. This transparency reduces confusion for returning members who may have been navigating the full purchase funnel unnecessarily, unsure whether they needed to buy a ticket at all. The streamlined navigation and shortened funnel also benefit members disproportionately, as they are likely repeat visitors who previously had to re-navigate the same lengthy browsing path on each return visit.

How This Connects to the Data

The lower member conversion rate suggested friction specific to returning users. By clarifying membership benefits at the point of purchase and reducing navigation overhead for repeat visitors, we address the likely causes of member drop-off without introducing a separate, complex member-specific flow.

5. Design Summary

Visual Direction

The redesign adopts an elegant, modern aesthetic with a neutral black-white-gray palette accented by blue. Headings use Instrument Serif for a refined, editorial quality. Subheadings and body text use Roboto in varying weights and cases to create clear typographic hierarchy. This visual system communicates the sophistication of a contemporary art museum while maintaining the clarity and scannability needed for efficient task completion.

Information Architecture

The site was restructured around a single guiding principle: reduce the distance between landing and purchasing. The persistent navigation bar provides consistent orientation across all pages. The home page hero section serves as both a brand introduction and a direct purchase entry point. Content pages like Explore and Exhibitions are streamlined to deliver value without creating browsing loops that delay conversion.

Key Metrics I Expect to Improve

  • Funnel step count reduced from ~10 to ~3-4 steps, approaching the 5.1-step industry benchmark
  • Ticket purchase discoverability improved through persistent navigation and hero placement
  • Member confusion reduced through clear benefit communication at point of purchase
  • Overall conversion rate targeted to improve beyond the current 9.45% baseline
